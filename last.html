INTERESTING

Another example of the use of blue is in the "Blue Laws". These laws though not nearly as popular in this country now as they were in the past, closed all or most businesses on Sundays and the part most of of us are familiar with, included no alcohol serving or buying. Or at least not before noon on Sundays.

I'm not sure how the word "Blue" was incorporated into the name. Perhaps because the early Puritans were feeling "blue" with no beer or ale to quench their thirst. Remember that the Puritans primarily drank beer and ale because water was not purified or always safe to drink as it is now. This made beer and ale which were safer, basically because of the alcohol content, their default drinks.

Or it may come from the BlueLaws, which in U.S. history, is a law forbidding certain secular activities on Sunday. The name may derive from Samuel A. Peters's General History of Connecticut (1781), which purported to list the stiff Sabbath regulations at New Haven, Connecticut; the work was printed on blue paper. A more probable derivation is based on an 18th-century usage of the word blue meaning "rigidly moral" in a disparaging sense. Strictest in Puritan, Bible-oriented communities.*

* https://www.britannica.com/topic/blue-law

Now we all have a little more to think about whenever we see the color blue.
